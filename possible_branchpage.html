Will Arms Treaty Hurt Privacy?
by James Glave

4:45 p.m.  4.Dec.98.PST
A Canadian privacy-software firm is concerned that a new international arms-trafficking agreement will undermine Canada's electronic commerce and consumer privacy around the world.

On Thursday, 33 nations including Canada, the United States, and Britain agreed to tighten controls on data-scrambling technologies as part of a new draft of the Wassenaar Arrangement, an arms-control treaty. The arrangement restricts strong data-scrambling technology under the premise that it might be used by terrorists.

But it also affects a company called Zero Knowledge Systems, which is building a product called Freedom that will allow Net users to completely conceal their identity.

"The US venture-capital firms are going nuts for [our product], and we have a potential market of 120 million people," said Zero Knowledge Systems president Austin Hill. "And now the Canadian government with these new rules is saying that could be halved because the US government does not have an inherent interest in seeing this software come out."

"The United States has been trying to maintain restrictive cryptography policies at home," said Hill. "Their own industry has been lobbying and fighting to remove these restrictions. The US government's answer to this is to 'level the playing field' by imposing the same ridiculous controls on other countries through foreign trade agreements."

The new Wassenaar Arrangement cuts out provisions in the previous version that allowed for the export of strong mass-market crypto. Canada adhered to this aspect of the arrangement while the United States did not -- creating a trade advantage that allowed Canadian security companies to flourish.

The new agreement states the change was made to recognize "the modernization of encryption controls to keep pace with developing technology and electronic commerce, while also being mindful of security interests."

That means Canadian companies developing high-grade crypto will likely need to obtain export licenses, effectively curtailing an advantage enjoyed by the Canadian software industry.

Some critics charge that the US Department of Commerce bullied other countries in the talks. The new Wassenaar Arrangement tightens controls even though only three of the 33 countries have publicly endorsed that position.

Industry Canada, the arm of government overseeing electronic-commerce policy, could not be reached for comment.

Hill said he has invested millions in the product Freedom, which depends on Canada's relaxed approach to high-grade crypto export. He will speak at CIPHER 99, a conference late next year devoted to the technology's importance to the protection of human rights.

"All of this is up in the air on whether or not we can launch," he said by phone from Montreal, Quebec.

One privacy-policy expert said that Hill's business model depended too strongly on a fluid political situation.

"We are going to have to have a model for an identity infrastructure that works but also is conscious of where everything is going with export issues," said the source, who spoke on condition of anonymity.

"We have to develop a technology that is aware of the political environment," the source added.
